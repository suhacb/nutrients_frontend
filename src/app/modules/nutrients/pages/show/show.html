Micronutrient Vitamin

Description

Purpose / Role in the Human Body

Vitamin D₄ is a less-common form of the vitamin D family. It is also named 22‑dihydroergocalciferol.

The more familiar forms — Vitamin D₃ (cholecalciferol) and Vitamin D₂ (ergocalciferol) — are well-studied: they help the body absorb calcium and phosphate, support bone mineralisation and general musculoskeletal and immune health.

For vitamin D₄: It is documented as a “vitamin D” compound present in some fungi/mushrooms, but its specific physiological role in humans is not well defined. The body of research on D₄ is much smaller compared to D₂ or D₃.

Because it is structurally similar to other calciferols, it could contribute to vitamin D activity (i.e., calcium/phosphate regulation, bone health) but whether it’s absorbed or metabolised to the same degree or has the same potency is still unclear.

Bottom line: If you see Vitamin D₄, treat it as a member of the vitamin D family, but understand that the evidence for it in humans is limited. Most guidelines refer to D₂/D₃.

Recommended Daily Amount for a Healthy Adult

Since most guidelines refer to vitamin D in general (D₂ + D₃) and not specifically D₄, there is no standard recommended daily allowance (RDA) for vitamin D₄ alone.

For vitamin D generally: The U.S. Institute of Medicine/NIH points to about 600 IU (15 µg) per day for adults up to ~70 years, and 800 IU (20 µg) for older adults.

Therefore for Vitamin D₄, one cannot confidently assign a separate value: you'd follow the general vitamin D intake guidelines unless a specialist provides different advice.

Main Ingredient Sources (Where D₄ Comes From)

Vitamin D₄ is found in certain mushrooms: it’s produced from a precursor called ergosta-5,7-dienol (22,23-dihydroergosterol) rather than the typical ergosterol → D₂ pathway.

Because D₄ is relatively rare (and less documented in many common foods), most of the “vitamin D” in your diet/sun-exposure will be D₂ or D₃ rather than D₄.

Common sources for vitamin D overall (mostly D₂/D₃) include: fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines), egg yolks, fortified milks/cereals, and sunlight exposure.

For mushrooms specifically: Some types when exposed to UV light can produce D₂ and possibly D₄, but data is limited.
